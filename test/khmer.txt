<p><b>Khmer</b> (ភាសាខ្មែរ), or <b>Cambodian</b>, is the language of the <a href="/wiki/Khmer_people" title="Khmer people">Khmer people</a> and the official language of <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>. One of the more prominent <a href="/wiki/Austroasiatic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Austroasiatic languages">Austroasiatic languages</a> with speakers in the tens of millions, it is the second most widely spoken Austroasiatic language. Khmer has been considerably influenced by <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> and <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81li" class="mw-redirect" title="Pāli">Pali</a>, especially in the royal and religious <a href="/wiki/Register_%28linguistics%29" class="mw-redirect" title="Register (linguistics)">registers</a>, through the vehicles of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>. It is also the earliest recorded and earliest written language of the <a href="/wiki/Mon-Khmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Mon-Khmer">Mon-Khmer</a> family, predating <a href="/wiki/Mon_language" title="Mon language">Mon</a> and by a significant margin <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_Language" class="mw-redirect" title="Vietnamese Language">Vietnamese</a>. As a result of geographic proximity, the Khmer language has affected, and also been affected by, <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lao_language" title="Lao language">Lao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cham_language" title="Cham language">Cham</a> which all form a <a href="/wiki/Sprachbund" title="Sprachbund">sprachbund</a> in peninsular <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cl_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cl-1" title="">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Khmer has its own script, an <a href="/wiki/Abugida" title="Abugida">abugida</a> known in Khmer as <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Script" class="mw-redirect" title="Khmer Script">Aksar Khmer</a>.</p>
<p>Khmer differs from neighboring languages such as Thai, Lao and Vietnamese in that it is not a <a href="/wiki/Tonal_language" title="Tonal language">tonal language</a>. All its main dialects that are mutually intelligible:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Battambang</b>, spoken in northern Cambodia.</li>
<li><b>Phnom Penh</b>, the capital dialect and is also spoken in surrounding provinces.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Northern_Khmer" title="Northern Khmer">Northern Khmer</a></b>, also known as <i>Khmer Surin</i>, spoken by ethnic Khmer native to Northeast Thailand</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Khmer_Krom" title="Khmer Krom">Khmer Krom</a></b> or <b>Southern Khmer</b>, spoken by the indigenous Khmer population of the Mekong Delta.</li>
<li><b>Cardamom Khmer</b>, an archaic form spoken by a small population in the <a href="/wiki/Cardamom_Mountains" title="Cardamom Mountains">Cardamom Mountains</a> of western Cambodia.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2" title="">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p><a name="History" id="History"></a></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Khmer_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">History</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">Linguistic</a> study of the Khmer language divides its history into four periods.<sup id="cite_ref-SIDWELL_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SIDWELL-3" title="">[4]</a></sup> Pre-Angkorian Khmer, the language after its divergence from Proto-Mon-Khmer until the ninth century, is only known from words and phrases in Sanskrit texts of the era. Old Khmer (or Angkorian Khmer) is the language as it was spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Empire" title="Khmer Empire">Khmer Empire</a> from the 9th century until the weakening of the empire sometime in the 13th century. Old Khmer is attested by many primary sources and has been studied in depth by a few scholars, most notably Saveros Pou, Phillip Jenner and Heinz-Jürgen Pinnow. Following the end of the Khmer Empire the language lost the standardizing influence of being the language of government and accordingly underwent a turbulent period of change in <a href="/wiki/Morphology_%28linguistics%29" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a>. The language of this transition period, from about the 14th to 18th centuries, is referred to as Middle Khmer and saw borrowing from Thai, Lao and, to a lesser extent, Vietnamese. The changes during this period are so profound that the rules of Modern Khmer can not be applied to correctly understand the Old Khmer. The language became recognizable as the Modern Khmer spoken today in the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-SIDWELL_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SIDWELL-3" title="">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Khmer is classified as a member of the Eastern branch of the <a href="/wiki/Mon-Khmer_languages" title="Mon-Khmer languages">Mon-Khmer</a> language family, itself a subdivision of the larger Austro-Asiatic language group, which has representatives in a large swath of land from Northeast India down through Southeast Asia to the Malay Peninsula and its islands. As such, its closest relatives are the languages of the <a href="/wiki/Pearic_languages" title="Pearic languages">Pearic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bahnaric_languages" title="Bahnaric languages">Bahnaric</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Katuic_languages" title="Katuic languages">Katuic</a> families spoken by the hill tribes of the region.<sup id="cite_ref-Shorto_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shorto-4" title="">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Vietic_languages" title="Vietic languages">Vietic languages</a> have also been classified as belonging to this family.</p>

<p>Syllables begin with one of these consonants or consonant clusters, followed by one of the vowel nuclei. When the vowel nucleus is short, there has to be a final consonant. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/p, t, c, k, ʔ, m, n, ɲ, ŋ, l, h, j, ʋ/</span> can exist in a syllable coda. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/h/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʋ/</span> become <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ç]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[w]</span> respectively. The most common word structure in Khmer is a full syllable as described above, preceded by an unstressed, “minor” syllable that has a consonant-vowel (CV) structure CV-, CrV-, CVN- or CrVN- (N is any nasal in the Khmer inventory). Words can also be made up of two full syllables. The vowel in these preceding syllables is usually reduced in conversation to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ə]</span>, however in careful or formal speech and in TV and radio, they are always clearly articulated.</p>
<p>Words with three or more syllables exist, particularly those pertaining to science, the arts, and religion. These words are loanwords, usually derived from Pali, Sanskrit, or more recently, French.</p>
<p><a name="Grammar" id="Grammar"></a></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Khmer_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Grammar">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Grammar</span></h2>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="noprint relarticle mainarticle"><i>Main article: <a href="/wiki/Khmer_grammar" title="Khmer grammar">Khmer grammar</a></i></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Khmer is generally a <a href="/wiki/Subject_Verb_Object" title="Subject Verb Object">Subject Verb Object</a> (SVO) language with <a href="/wiki/Prepositions" class="mw-redirect" title="Prepositions">prepositions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6" title="">[7]</a></sup> Although primarily an <a href="/wiki/Isolating_language" title="Isolating language">isolating language</a>, lexical <a href="/wiki/Derivation_%28linguistics%29" title="Derivation (linguistics)">derivation</a> by means of <a href="/wiki/Prefixes" class="mw-redirect" title="Prefixes">prefixes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infixes" class="mw-redirect" title="Infixes">infixes</a> is common.<sup id="cite_ref-name_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-name-7" title="">[8]</a></sup> Adjectives, demonstratives and numerals follow their noun:</p>
<p>ស្រីឡើនោះ <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/srəːj</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">lʔɐː</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">nuç/</span> (girl pretty that) = that pretty girl</p>
<p>The noun has no grammatical gender or singular/plural distinction. Plurality can be marked by <a href="/wiki/Postnominal" class="mw-redirect" title="Postnominal">postnominal</a> particles, numerals, or by doubling the adjective, which can also serve to intensify the adjective:</p>
<p>ឆ្កែធំ <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/cʰkae</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">tʰom/</span> (dog large) = large dog</p>
<p>ឆ្កែធំធំ <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/cʰkae</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">tʰom</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">tʰom/</span> (dog large large) = large dogs or a very large dog</p>
<p>ឆ្កែពីរ <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/cʰkae</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">piː/</span> (dog two) = two dogs</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Classifier_%28linguistics%29" title="Classifier (linguistics)">Classifying particles</a> for use between numerals and nouns exist although are not obligatory as in, for example, Thai. As is typical of most East Asian languages,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8" title="">[9]</a></sup> the verb does not inflect at all; tense and aspect can be shown by particles and adverbs or understood by context. Verbs are negated by putting "<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/min/</span>", "<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/pum/</span>" or "<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʔɐt/</span>" before them and "<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/teː/</span>" at the end of the sentence or clause.</p>
<p>ខ្ញុំជឿ <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/kʰɲom</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">cɨə/</span> - I believe</p>
<p>ខ្ញុំមិនជឿទេ <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/kʰɲom</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">min</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">cɨə</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">teː/</span> - I don't believe</p>
<p><a name="Dialects" id="Dialects"></a></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Khmer_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dialects">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Dialects</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">Dialects</a> are sometimes quite marked. Notable variations are found in speakers from <a href="/wiki/Phnom_Penh" title="Phnom Penh">Phnom Penh</a> (the capital city), the rural <a href="/wiki/Battambang_Province" title="Battambang Province">Battambang</a> area, the areas of Northeast Thailand adjacent to Cambodia such as <a href="/wiki/Surin_province" class="mw-redirect" title="Surin province">Surin province</a>, the Cardamom Mountains, and in southern Vietnam.<sup id="cite_ref-SIDWELL_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SIDWELL-3" title="">[4]</a></sup> The dialects form a <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">continuum</a> running roughly north to south. The speech of Phnom Penh, considered the standard, is mutually intelligible with the others but a <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Krom" title="Khmer Krom">Khmer Krom</a> speaker from Vietnam, for instance, may have great difficulty communicating with a Khmer native to <a href="/wiki/Sisaket_Province" title="Sisaket Province">Sisaket Province</a> in Thailand.</p>
<p>Northern Khmer, the dialect spoken in Thailand, is referred to in Khmer as <i>Khmer Surin</i> and, although it only began divergence from standard Khmer within the last 200 years, is considered by some linguists to be a separate language. This is due to its distinct accent influenced by the surrounding tonal language, <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>, lexical differences and its phonemic differences in both vowels and distribution of consonants. Final "r", which has become silent in other dialects of Khmer, is pronounced in Northern Khmer.</p>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Khmer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Western Khmer (page does not exist)">Western Khmer</a>, also called <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cardamom_Khmer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cardamom Khmer (page does not exist)">Cardamom Khmer</a>, spoken by a small, isolated population in the Cardamom mountain range extending from Cambodia into Thailand, although little studied, is unique in that it maintains a definite system of <a href="/wiki/Register_%28phonology%29" title="Register (phonology)">vocal register</a> that has all but disappeared in other dialects of modern Khmer.<sup id="cite_ref-SIDWELL_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SIDWELL-3" title="">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A notable characteristic of Phnom Penh casual speech is merging or complete <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">elision</a> of syllables, considered by speakers from other regions as a "relaxed" pronunciation. For instance, "Phnom Penh" will sometimes be shortened to "m'Penh". Another characteristic of Phnom Penh speech is observed in words with an "r" either as an initial consonant or as the second member of a <a href="/wiki/Consonant_cluster" title="Consonant cluster">consonant cluster</a> (as in the English word "bread"). The "r", <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">trilled</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_tap" title="Alveolar tap">flapped</a> in other dialects, is either pronounced as an <a href="/wiki/Uvular_trill" title="Uvular trill">uvular trill</a> (similar to French) or not pronounced at all. This alters the quality of any preceding consonant causing a harder, more emphasized pronunciation. Another unique result is that the syllable is spoken with a low-rising or "dipping" <a href="/wiki/Tone_%28linguistics%29" title="Tone (linguistics)">tone</a> much like the "hỏi" tone in Northern <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>. For example, some people pronounce <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/trəj/</span> (meaning "fish") as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/təj/</span>, the "r" is dropped and the vowel begins by dipping much lower in tone than standard speech and then rises, effectively doubling its length. Another example is the word <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/riən/</span> ("study, learn"). It is pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʀiən/</span>, with the "uvular r" and the same intonation described above.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9" title="">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a name="Social_registers" id="Social_registers"></a></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Khmer_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Social registers">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Social registers</span></h2>
<p>Khmer employs a system of <a href="/wiki/Register_%28linguistics%29" class="mw-redirect" title="Register (linguistics)">registers</a> in which the speaker must always be conscious of the social status of the person spoken to. The different registers, which include those used for common speech, polite speech, speaking to or about royals and speaking to or about monks, employ alternate verbs, names of body parts and pronouns. This results in what appears to foreigners as separate languages and, in fact, isolated villagers often are unsure how to speak with royals and royals raised completely within the court do not feel comfortable speaking the common register. Another result is that the pronominal system is complex and full of honorific variations.</p>
<p>As an example, the word for "to eat" used between intimates or in reference to animals is <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/siː/</span>. Used in polite reference to commoners, it's <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɲam/</span>. When used of those of higher social status, it's <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/pisa/</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/tɔtuəl tiən/</span>. For monks the word is <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/cʰan/</span> and for royals, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/saoj/</span><sup id="cite_ref-cl_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cl-1" title="">[2]</a></sup>.</p>
<p><a name="Writing_system" id="Writing_system"></a></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Khmer_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Writing system">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline">Writing system</span></h2>
<dl>
<dd>
<div class="noprint relarticle mainarticle"><i>Main article: <a href="/wiki/Khmer_script" title="Khmer script">Khmer script</a></i></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Khmer is written with the <a href="/wiki/Khmer_script" title="Khmer script">Khmer script</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Abugida" title="Abugida">abugida</a> developed from the <a href="/wiki/Pallava_script" class="mw-redirect" title="Pallava script">Pallava script</a> of India before the 7th century.<sup id="cite_ref-OMNI_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OMNI-10" title="">[11]</a></sup> The Khmer script is similar in appearance and usage to both <a href="/wiki/Thai_script" class="mw-redirect" title="Thai script">Thai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lao_script" title="Lao script">Lao</a> which were based on the Khmer system.<sup id="cite_ref-OMNI_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OMNI-10" title="">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Khmer_numerals" title="Khmer numerals">Khmer numerals</a>, which were inherited from Indian numerals, are used more widely than <a href="/wiki/Hindu-Arabic_numerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu-Arabic numerals">Hindu-Arabic numerals</a>. The Khmer script is also used within Cambodia to transcribe hill tribe languages that have no writing system.<sup id="cite_ref-HUFF_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HUFF-5" title="">[6]</a></sup></p>
